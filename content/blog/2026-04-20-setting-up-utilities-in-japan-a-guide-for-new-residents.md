# Setting Up Utilities in Japan: A Guide for New Residents

Moving into your new Japanese apartment is exciting — until you realize the lights won't turn on, there's no gas for your stove, and you're not sure who to call. Unlike many Western countries where utilities transfer automatically with a new lease, Japan requires you to personally arrange each service. The good news? Once you understand the system, setting up utilities in Japan is straightforward, even with limited Japanese.

This guide walks you through everything you need to know about connecting electricity, gas, water, and internet in your new Japanese home — including real costs, timelines, and the exact steps to get everything running.

## Understanding Japan's Utility System

In Japan, utilities are typically handled separately from your rental contract. Your landlord or property management company won't set these up for you — it's the tenant's responsibility. This catches many newcomers off guard, especially those from countries where utilities are often included in rent or automatically transferred.

The four main utilities you'll need to arrange are:

- **Electricity (電気 / denki)**
- **Gas (ガス / gasu)**
- **Water (水道 / suidō)**
- **Internet (インターネット)**

Most utility companies have English support lines or online registration, though the level of service varies by region. Working with a bilingual real estate agent can make this process significantly smoother, as many agencies help coordinate initial utility setup as part of their service to foreign clients.

## Electricity: The Easiest to Set Up

### How to Connect

Electricity is usually the simplest utility to arrange. In many apartments, power is already connected — you just need to register your name with the local electric company.

Look for the **breaker box (ブレーカー / burēkā)** near your entrance. Flip the main switch to "ON." If power comes on, you're halfway there. Next, find the postcard or registration form left by the previous tenant or utility company, fill it out, and mail it in.

Alternatively, call or register online. Major providers include:

- **TEPCO** (Tokyo Electric Power Company) — Kanto region
- **Kansai Electric** — Osaka, Kyoto, Kobe area
- **Chubu Electric** — Nagoya region

Since Japan deregulated its electricity market in 2016, you can also choose alternative providers that may offer lower rates or bundle deals with gas.

### Typical Costs

For a one-bedroom apartment, expect to pay **¥3,000–¥8,000/month ($20–$55 USD)** depending on usage and season. Air conditioning in summer and heating in winter will spike your bills significantly — Japanese homes are notoriously poorly insulated.

## Gas: Requires an In-Person Appointment

### City Gas vs. Propane

Japan has two types of gas service:

- **City gas (都市ガス / toshi gasu)** — piped directly to your home, common in urban areas
- **Propane/LP gas (プロパンガス)** — delivered in tanks, common in suburban and rural areas

Propane is typically **30–50% more expensive** than city gas, so this is worth checking before you sign a lease.

### How to Connect

Unlike electricity, **gas requires an in-person appointment**. A technician must visit to open the gas valve, check for leaks, and ensure your appliances are compatible. You'll need to be home for this — appointments usually take 15–30 minutes.

Call your local gas company at least **3–5 days before move-in** to schedule. Tokyo Gas and Osaka Gas both have English support lines.

### Typical Costs

City gas for a single person runs approximately **¥2,000–¥5,000/month ($14–$35 USD)**. Propane users should budget **¥5,000–¥10,000/month ($35–$70 USD)** or more.

## Water: Contact Your Local Ward Office

### How to Connect

Water is managed by municipal governments, not private companies. Contact your local **ward office (区役所 / kuyakusho)** or **city office (市役所 / shiyakusho)** to start service.

In some buildings, water is already running when you move in — you simply need to register. Look for paperwork in your mailbox or near the water meter.

### Typical Costs

Water bills in Japan are remarkably affordable. Expect **¥2,000–¥4,000/month ($14–$28 USD)** for typical usage. Bills usually come every two months.

## Internet: Plan Ahead for Installation

### Your Options

Internet setup takes the longest — often **2–4 weeks** for installation — so start this process early.

Your main options are:

- **Fiber optic (光回線 / hikari kaisen)** — fastest and most reliable, providers include NTT Flets, NURO, and au Hikari
- **Pocket WiFi (モバイルWiFi)** — portable, no installation required, good temporary solution
- **Home router plans** — uses mobile networks, easier setup than fiber

Many apartment buildings have existing fiber infrastructure, which speeds up installation. Ask your property manager which providers service your building.

### Typical Costs

Fiber internet runs **¥4,000–¥6,000/month ($28–$42 USD)**. Installation fees range from **¥15,000–¥40,000 ($100–$275 USD)**, though campaigns often waive these costs for new subscribers.

## Pro Tips for a Smooth Setup

### Before Move-In Day

1. **Get your move-in date confirmed in writing** — you'll need this for utility appointments
2. **Schedule gas service at least one week early** — appointments fill up, especially at month-end
3. **Order internet immediately after signing your lease** — the wait time is real
4. **Ask your real estate agent for help** — reputable agencies assisting foreign clients often provide utility setup support or translated guides

### Payment Methods

Most utilities can be paid via:

- **Bank auto-transfer (口座振替 / kōza furikae)** — set up at your bank
- **Credit card** — not all providers accept this
- **Convenience store payment (コンビニ払い)** — take the bill slip to any konbini

Auto-transfer is most convenient and sometimes earns small discounts.

### What If You Don't Speak Japanese?

Major utility companies in Tokyo, Osaka, and other large cities have English support. For smaller cities or propane providers, you may need help. This is another area where having a bilingual real estate agent proves invaluable — they can make calls on your behalf and ensure nothing gets lost in translation.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Forgetting to cancel utilities when moving out** — you'll keep getting billed
- **Missing the gas appointment** — rescheduling can take another week
- **Assuming utilities are included in rent** — they almost never are in Japan
- **Waiting too long for internet** — you might spend your first month tethering to your phone
