# Japan Golden Visa and Property Ownership for Investors: What You Need to Know in 2025

Japan has long been a dream destination for international investors — a stable economy, world-class infrastructure, and property prices that remain surprisingly accessible compared to other G7 nations. But unlike Portugal or Spain, Japan hasn't historically offered a straightforward "buy property, get residency" pathway.

That changed in April 2025, when Japan officially launched its new **Golden Visa program** (正式には「特別高度人材制度」), creating a clearer route for high-net-worth individuals to gain long-term residency. If you're an investor from the US, Australia, the UK, or Canada considering Japanese real estate, here's what you need to know about combining property ownership with residency goals.

## What Is Japan's Golden Visa Program?

Japan's Golden Visa — officially part of the expanded **Highly Skilled Professional (HSP) visa framework** — allows qualifying investors to obtain residency based on their assets, income, and contributions to Japan's economy. Unlike traditional work visas, this pathway doesn't require employment with a Japanese company.

### Key Eligibility Requirements

To qualify under the investor track, you'll generally need to meet these thresholds:

- **Minimum assets**: ¥100 million (approximately $670,000 USD) in investable assets
- **Annual income**: ¥20 million+ (around $135,000 USD) from investments or business activities
- **Investment commitment**: Demonstrable investment in Japanese assets, which can include real estate, securities, or business ventures
- **Clean background**: No criminal record and standard immigration health requirements

**Important note**: Simply buying property alone does not automatically qualify you. The program evaluates your overall financial profile, investment activity, and potential contribution to Japan's economy through a points-based system.

## Does Buying Property in Japan Help You Get Residency?

Here's where many investors get confused. **Property ownership itself is not a visa category in Japan** — anyone, regardless of nationality, can purchase real estate without any residency requirement. A Canadian living in Toronto can buy a Tokyo apartment tomorrow without stepping foot in Japan.

However, real estate investment *can* strengthen your Golden Visa application in several ways:

### 1. Demonstrates Financial Commitment
Owning substantial Japanese property (typically ¥50 million or more) shows immigration authorities you have genuine ties to Japan and aren't simply seeking a visa of convenience.

### 2. Supports Business Manager Visa Applications
If you structure your investment as a **property management company**, you may qualify for a **Business Manager Visa (経営・管理ビザ)**. This requires:
- Establishing a Japanese corporation (株式会社 or 合同会社)
- Initial capital of at least ¥5 million ($33,500 USD)
- A physical office address in Japan
- A viable business plan showing profitability

Many foreign investors use this route, purchasing multiple rental properties and managing them through their own company.

### 3. Generates Qualifying Income
Rental income from Japanese properties counts toward the income requirements for various visa categories, including the Golden Visa's ¥20 million threshold.

## Practical Steps to Combine Property Investment with Residency

If you're serious about both investing in Japanese real estate and establishing residency, here's a realistic roadmap:

### Step 1: Purchase Property (No Visa Required)

Start by acquiring property that matches your investment goals. Popular options include:

- **Central Tokyo apartments**: ¥30-80 million ($200,000-535,000 USD) for rentable 1-2 bedroom units in areas like Shibuya, Shinjuku, or Minato
- **Osaka investment properties**: ¥15-40 million ($100,000-270,000 USD) with higher rental yields than Tokyo
- **Vacation rentals in Niseko or Hakuba**: ¥50-150 million+ ($335,000-1,000,000 USD) for ski-in/ski-out properties with strong seasonal income

When purchasing, you'll encounter Japan-specific costs including:
- **Shikikin (敷金)**: Security deposit, typically 1-2 months' rent equivalent
- **Reikin (礼金)**: "Key money" or gift to the landlord — though this applies more to rentals, commercial purchases have similar customary fees
- **Real estate acquisition tax**: 3-4% of the assessed property value
- **Registration and stamp duties**: Approximately 1-2% combined

Working with a bilingual real estate agent who understands both Japanese market practices and foreign investor needs can save you significant time and prevent costly misunderstandings during this process.

### Step 2: Establish a Japanese Corporation (If Pursuing Business Manager Visa)

This typically costs ¥200,000-500,000 ($1,300-3,350 USD) in registration fees and requires:
- A registered office address (your property can qualify)
- Articles of incorporation drafted in Japanese
- A corporate bank account (increasingly accessible to foreign-owned companies)
- Appointment of at least one director (can be yourself)

### Step 3: Apply for Your Visa

With property owned and/or a business established, work with an immigration lawyer (行政書士 or 弁護士) to submit your application. Processing typically takes 1-3 months.

## What the Golden Visa Gets You

Successful applicants receive:

- **5-year residency status** (renewable)
- **Path to permanent residency**: Reduced from the standard 10 years to just 1-3 years for qualifying high-skilled professionals
- **Work authorization**: Freedom to engage in any business activity
- **Family inclusion**: Spouse and dependent children can obtain dependent visas

## Tax Considerations for Foreign Property Owners

Japan taxes rental income regardless of your residency status:

- **Non-residents**: 20.42% withholding tax on gross rental income
- **Residents**: Progressive income tax (5-45%) on net rental income, but with deductions for expenses, depreciation, and mortgage interest

Becoming a tax resident through the Golden Visa can actually reduce your overall tax burden if your properties generate significant income — another factor to discuss with a cross-border tax advisor.

## Is Japan's Golden Visa Right for You?

Japan's program suits investors who:

- Want genuine connection to Japan, not just a "passport in the drawer"
- Appreciate stable, long-term property appreciation over speculative gains
- Value quality of life factors like safety, healthcare, and infrastructure
- Have the financial resources to meet the ¥100 million asset threshold

It's less ideal if you're seeking the fastest or cheapest path to a second residency — countries like Portugal (until recently) or Malaysia offer lower barriers.
