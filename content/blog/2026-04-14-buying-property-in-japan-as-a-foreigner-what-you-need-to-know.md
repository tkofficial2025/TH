# Buying Property in Japan as a Foreigner: What You Need to Know

Japan is one of the few countries in the world where foreigners can buy property with virtually no restrictions — regardless of visa status or residency. Whether you're an expat living in Tokyo, an investor eyeing Osaka's rental market, or someone dreaming of a countryside retreat in Nagano, the Japanese real estate market is surprisingly accessible to international buyers.

But "accessible" doesn't mean "simple." The purchasing process involves unique Japanese practices, unfamiliar terminology, and potential financing hurdles that can catch first-time buyers off guard. This guide breaks down exactly what you need to know before buying property in Japan as a foreigner.

## Can Foreigners Actually Buy Property in Japan?

**Yes — with no legal restrictions.** Unlike countries such as Australia, Thailand, or Singapore, Japan places no limitations on foreign property ownership. You can buy land, apartments, houses, or commercial buildings regardless of whether you:

- Live in Japan or overseas
- Hold a visa or permanent residency
- Are an individual or purchasing through a company

You'll receive full ownership rights identical to Japanese citizens, including the ability to rent out, renovate, or resell the property. This openness has made Japan increasingly attractive to international investors, particularly from the US, Australia, Hong Kong, and Taiwan.

### The Catch: Financing Is Another Story

While ownership is unrestricted, **getting a mortgage as a foreigner is significantly harder**. Most Japanese banks require:

- Permanent residency (永住権 *eijūken*) or a Japanese spouse
- Stable employment history in Japan (typically 3+ years)
- The property to be your primary residence

Without permanent residency, your options narrow to a handful of banks willing to lend to visa holders — often with stricter terms, higher interest rates (1.5-3% vs. the standard 0.5-1%), and larger down payment requirements (30-50%).

**For non-resident foreign investors**, cash purchases are the norm. Some international banks like Shinsei Bank or Suruga Bank have historically offered loans to foreigners, though policies change frequently.

## Understanding the True Cost of Buying Property in Japan

The listing price is just the beginning. Budget for **6-10% in additional costs** on top of the purchase price:

### One-Time Purchase Costs

| Cost | Typical Amount |
|------|----------------|
| Real estate agent commission | 3% + ¥66,000 + tax (capped by law) |
| Registration and license tax | 1-2% of assessed value |
| Stamp duty | ¥10,000 - ¥60,000 |
| Judicial scrivener fees (司法書士) | ¥100,000 - ¥200,000 |
| Fire insurance | ¥20,000 - ¥50,000/year |

For a ¥50,000,000 property (~$335,000 USD), expect roughly ¥3,000,000-¥5,000,000 (~$20,000-$33,000) in additional closing costs.

### Ongoing Costs to Consider

- **Fixed asset tax (固定資産税 *kotei shisan zei*)**: ~1.4% of government-assessed value annually
- **City planning tax**: ~0.3% in urban areas
- **Management fees (管理費 *kanrihi*)**: ¥10,000-¥30,000/month for apartments
- **Repair reserve fund (修繕積立金 *shūzen tsumitatekin*)**: ¥5,000-¥20,000/month for apartments

These ongoing costs are particularly important for investment properties — they'll directly impact your rental yield calculations.

## The Property Buying Process: Step by Step

Working with a bilingual real estate agent who understands both Japanese practices and foreign buyer concerns makes this process significantly smoother. Here's what to expect:

### 1. Property Search and Viewing

Browse listings, shortlist properties, and arrange viewings. Unlike some Western markets, Japanese sellers rarely negotiate significantly on price — offers within 5% of asking are typical.

### 2. Purchase Application (買付申込書 *kaitsuke mōshikomisho*)

Submit a formal purchase application indicating your offer price. This isn't legally binding but shows serious intent and temporarily takes the property off market.

### 3. Important Matters Explanation (重要事項説明 *jūyō jikō setsumei*)

A licensed agent must explain all material facts about the property — including zoning, building regulations, and any defects. This legally mandated disclosure happens before signing and can take 1-2 hours.

### 4. Sales Contract (売買契約 *baibai keiyaku*)

Sign the contract and pay an earnest money deposit, typically **5-10% of the purchase price**. Walking away after this point means forfeiting your deposit.

### 5. Final Settlement and Registration

Pay the remaining balance, receive the keys, and register ownership at the Legal Affairs Bureau (法務局). A judicial scrivener handles the registration paperwork.

**Timeline**: From offer to ownership typically takes **1-3 months** for a cash purchase, longer if arranging financing.

## Key Considerations for Foreign Buyers

### New vs. Used Properties

Japan's real estate market is unusual — buildings depreciate rapidly while land retains value. A wooden house may be valued at nearly zero after 20-25 years, even if it's perfectly livable. This creates opportunities to buy "old" houses cheaply, but also means resale value can be unpredictable.

### Earthquake Resistance Standards

Properties built after **June 1981** meet modern seismic codes (新耐震基準 *shin taishin kijun*). Older buildings may require retrofitting. Always verify the construction date and earthquake compliance.

### Leasehold vs. Freehold Land

Most properties include freehold land ownership (所有権 *shoyūken*), but some — particularly in prime Tokyo locations — are built on leasehold land (借地権 *shakuchiken*). Leasehold properties are cheaper upfront but involve ongoing ground rent and renewal negotiations.

### Akiya (Empty Houses)

Japan's countryside is dotted with **akiya** (空き家) — abandoned homes available for extremely low prices, sometimes under ¥1,000,000 ($7,000). These require significant renovation and often come with hidden costs, but represent unique opportunities for adventurous buyers.

## Practical Tips for Success

1. **Get your finances in order first** — Know whether you're paying cash or need to secure financing before seriously shopping.

2. **Hire a bilingual agent** — Contracts and negotiations happen in Japanese. Miscommunication can be costly.

3. **Verify building management health** — For apartments, review the management association's finances and repair history.

4. **Consider tax implications in both countries** — Consult tax professionals familiar with your home country's treatment of foreign real estate.

5. **Visit in person if possible** — Photos can hide neighborhood noise, access issues, or building conditions.
